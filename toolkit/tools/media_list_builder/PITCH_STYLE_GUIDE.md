# Pitch Style Guide

## What a pitch is

A pitch is not a press release, a briefing document, or a story summary. It is a one-paragraph note that makes a specific journalist want to make one specific phone call. It should answer three questions in under 150 words: Why you? Why me? Why now?

## Length

- Target: 100–180 words in the body
- Hard cap: 200 words. If you need more, you have not yet found the angle.
- Short paragraphs. No walls of text.

## Structure

1. Subject line — specific, signal-clear, no hype
2. Personalized opener — one sentence tying to a specific story the reporter wrote
3. The development — what specifically happened, changed, or is imminent
4. Why it matters to their audience — one sentence, not a paragraph
5. One concrete offer — interview, source, data, background call, or event access
6. Light CTA — one sentence, not three asks

## Subject line rules

- State the angle or development, not the topic category
- Bad: "Story idea on AI regulation"
- Bad: "Timely update — emerging policy landscape"
- Good: "Comment period closes May 30 — FTC data broker rule"
- Good: "New state filing: [Entity] challenges [specific provision]"
- No exclamation marks. No "EXCLUSIVE." No rhetorical questions.

## Opening rules

- Reference a specific article the reporter wrote — title, approximate date, what it covered
- One sentence. Do not over-flatter. Do not recap their story back to them.
- Move immediately into the pitch
- Bad: "I've been following your excellent work on healthcare policy and wanted to reach out."
- Good: "You covered the Senate's DOGE hearing in March — there's a follow worth flagging."

## The development (why now)

- Must name a specific trigger: a date, deadline, vote, ruling, filing, appointment, hearing, or newly released document
- If no specific trigger exists, do not fake urgency. State what has changed and why it is relevant now.
- Bad triggers (do not use):
  - "emerging legislative language"
  - "timely update on the regulatory landscape"
  - "updating consensus around this issue"
  - "a brief briefing on recent developments"
  - "growing interest in this space"
  - "this issue is gaining traction"
- Good triggers:
  - "The House passed [bill] on Tuesday — the Senate vote is scheduled for next week."
  - "The comment period closes May 30."
  - "The ruling came down yesterday."
  - "The agency released a 200-page report this morning."
  - "The hearing is Thursday."

## Value offer rules

Offer ONE thing. Not three. The offer must be grounded in something real:
- Named expert or senior official available for interview
- Access to a filing, data set, or document
- On-record comment or statement
- Background call with a knowledgeable source
- Event access (with specific logistics if known)

Do NOT offer:
- Exclusive data unless you actually have it
- Embargoed reports unless they exist
- Access you cannot guarantee
- A "briefing" without specifying who gives it and on what

## Tone

- Professional but human. Write as one person to another.
- Direct. Get to the point in the first sentence after the opener.
- Confident but not pushy. One ask, clearly stated.
- No brand adjectives ("groundbreaking," "unprecedented," "game-changing")
- No "I think this could be a great fit for your readers" — either it is or it isn't

## What to avoid

These phrases are signals that the pitch is not ready:

| Do not write | Write instead |
|---|---|
| "emerging legislative language" | Name the bill, provision, or agency rule |
| "timely update" | State the specific event and date |
| "updating consensus" | Say what changed and who changed it |
| "brief briefing" | Specify: call with [Name], [Title] |
| "this issue is gaining traction" | Name the vote, hearing, or filing |
| "given recent policy developments" | Name the development |
| "I wanted to flag this for you" | Lead with the development itself |
| "I think this would resonate with your readers" | Cut it entirely |
| "We have exciting news" | Cut it entirely |

## Calibration use of examples

Examples are reference only — for length calibration, specificity level, and how quickly the pitch gets to the point.

Do not copy their framing, cadence, sentence structure, or sign-off style.
