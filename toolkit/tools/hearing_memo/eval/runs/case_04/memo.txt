And Ms. Gibson, you mentioned it as well in your testimony.

Hearing Overview
The None held a hearing titled "And Ms. Gibson, you mentioned it as well in your testimony.". Key themes included national security implications, supply chain vulnerabilities and domestic manufacturing, pharmaceutical oversight and drug safety, legislative and policy reform proposals, oversight and transparency measures. The committee heard testimony from 5 witnesses offering perspectives on the issues under examination. Members engaged witnesses on policy implications, legislative responses, and areas requiring further oversight.

Committee Leadership Opening Statements
Witnesses Introductions and Testimonies
Rosemary Gibson
Mr./Ms. Gibson Mr. Gibson argued that the Department of Veterans Affairs, HHS, and the Department of War should utilize data funded by the Department of War to inform procurement decisions, emphasizing the importance of purchasing based on quality to encourage the acquisition of products from the United States and other quality-conscious countries. He identified risks associated with sourcing generics from countries like China and India, where compliance with U.S. laws is often lacking, and noted that a small number of companies dominate the sourcing of generics, prioritizing cost over quality. Gibson recommended that U.S. CEOs should be convened to shift their sourcing strategies away from a sole focus on cost to include quality considerations, leveraging existing testing data to improve the overall safety and efficacy of medicines distributed in the country.

Chan Harjivan
Mr./Ms. Harjivan testified that the pharmaceutical supply chain can be improved without incurring significant costs by implementing longer-term contracts that provide visibility into the supply chain, which would enhance predictability of demand. They highlighted the risks associated with a lack of transparency in drug sourcing, noting that consumers are often unaware of the origins and quality of medications compared to other products, such as automobiles. Harjivan emphasized the importance of diversifying suppliers to mitigate quality issues, as reliance on single-source suppliers can lead to vulnerabilities. They recommended that Congress consider policies promoting transparent supply chains, supplier diversity, and the establishment of quality measures within long-term contracts to potentially lower the costs of generic medications, a view supported by discussions with generic manufacturers.

Rosemary Gibson
Mr./Ms. Gibson testified that the current generic drug testing program reveals that consumers are often paying more for lower-quality generics, while some of the less expensive options actually offer better quality. They identified the risk of consumers being misled by price alone, which could lead to health implications due to the consumption of subpar medications. To address these issues, Mr./Ms. Gibson recommended that Congress reevaluate the testing and pricing mechanisms for generic drugs to ensure that quality is prioritized over cost, thereby protecting consumer health and promoting fair market practices.

Gordon Chang
Mr./Ms. Chang testified that FDA rules and regulations currently pose a barrier to innovation in drug development, emphasizing the need for a balance between ensuring drug safety and efficacy while fostering innovation domestically. They identified the risk of losing pharmaceutical manufacturing to countries perceived as adversaries, such as China, if regulatory frameworks do not adapt to encourage local innovation. Mr./Ms. Chang recommended that Congress consider reforming FDA regulations to facilitate a more favorable environment for drug development in the United States, thereby preventing reliance on foreign manufacturing.

Ted Yoho
Mr./Ms. Yoho Mr. Yoho testified that the FDA should amend regulations to enhance accountability for pharmaceutical executives whose products fail to meet safety standards, emphasizing the need for corporate responsibility in drug safety for consumers and seniors. He identified significant national security and health risks associated with the dependence on China for pharmaceutical ingredients, particularly antibiotics, and highlighted the potential consequences of India's closure of its fermentation and fluorination processes. Additionally, he expressed concern over the brain drain of scientists and researchers to other countries, particularly China, and urged Congress to invest in the National Institutes of Health (NIH) and research initiatives to retain talent and maintain the United States' leadership in scientific innovation.

Q&A
Senator Rick Scott (R-FL)
Sen. Scott inquired about the challenges faced by the federal government in procuring American-made drugs, highlighting various departments involved. Witness Chan Harjivan responded that while these departments are significant purchasers, issues such as bureaucratic lethargy, entrenched suppliers, and a lack of understanding of procurement practices hinder progress; he emphasized the need for better education on procurement, transparency in supply chains, and awareness of economic dynamics to ensure that increased spending benefits American manufacturers without inflating prices unnecessarily.

Sen. Scott emphasized the importance of identifying leverage points in business, citing China's success in this regard. Witness Chan Harjivan responded by acknowledging that companies focus on maximizing profitability for shareholders, but suggested that metrics should be adjusted to enhance patient benefits.

Sen. Scott discussed the importance of being indispensable in business for better compensation. In response, Witness Chan Harjivan emphasized that companies are creating monopolies in the pharmaceutical industry and argued that government policy should prevent such monopolies.

Sen. Scott inquired about the comparison between China's economic coercion through pharmaceuticals and its use of rare earth elements or semiconductors. Witness Ted Yoho indicated that pharmaceuticals will increasingly play a significant role in China's leverage, referencing potential political maneuvers related to student visas and emphasizing the need for the U.S. to prepare for such scenarios, as similar tactics have already been employed with rare earth minerals.
