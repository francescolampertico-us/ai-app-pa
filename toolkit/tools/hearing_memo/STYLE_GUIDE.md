# Style Guide - Congressional Hearing Summary Memos

## 1. Purpose

This style guide defines the target style for congressional hearing summary memos.
The target output is the submitted house-style memo, not a generic hearing summary.

The memo should read like a polished internal policy product:
- formal
- clean
- easy to scan
- descriptive rather than argumentative
- compressed, but not telegraphic

## 2. Core output pattern

Default memo order:
1. FROM / DATE / SUBJECT block
2. standalone display title
3. Hearing Overview
4. Committee Leadership Opening Statements
5. Witnesses Introductions and Testimonies
6. Q&A
7. confidentiality footer

Do not add a separate "Closing Remarks" section unless the template family explicitly requires it.
If the chair's closing comments are material, fold them into the final chair subsection inside Q&A.

## 3. Tone

Use a tone that is:
- professional
- neutral
- concise
- analytical
- cleanly descriptive

Avoid a tone that is:
- chatty
- dramatic
- advocacy-driven
- academic
- generic-AI sounding
- too compressed to read naturally

## 4. Point of view

Write in third person.
Do not use first person.
Do not address the reader directly.
Do not use rhetorical questions.

## 5. Header block

The memo begins with exactly three metadata lines:
- FROM:
- DATE:
- SUBJECT:

Rules:
- labels are all caps and followed by a colon
- values should be plain text, no commentary
- FROM may be a person, team, or firm name
- SUBJECT should be formal and usually contain the committee name, hearing title, and hearing type if known
- preserve the submitted memo's preference for a long-form SUBJECT line rather than a shortened internal label

## 6. Display title

After the metadata block, repeat the official hearing title as a centered standalone title.

Rules:
- use the official hearing title when known
- do not embellish the title
- do not add the committee name to the display title unless it is part of the official title

## 7. Approved section headings

Default headings:
- Hearing Overview
- Committee Leadership Opening Statements
- Witnesses Introductions and Testimonies
- Q&A

Allowed alternate opening heading:
- Co-Chairs Opening Remarks

Rules:
- prefer the exact heading strings above
- do not invent near-synonyms such as "Leadership Remarks" or "Witness Testimony Summary"
- preserve the preferred house heading "Witnesses Introductions and Testimonies" unless a different approved family is intentionally selected

## 8. Paragraph rhythm

This memo family prefers short, controlled paragraphs.

Rules:
- most body paragraphs should be 2 to 4 sentences
- longer sections should usually be broken into two paragraphs instead of one large block
- each paragraph should carry one clear unit of meaning
- avoid transcript-like narration or line-by-line sequencing

## 9. Hearing Overview

Purpose:
- orient the reader quickly
- summarize the main themes of the hearing at a high level
- avoid witness-level detail unless absolutely necessary

Rules:
- one paragraph only
- usually 4 to 5 sentences
- name the committee, hearing title, date, and time
- summarize the issues discussed in broad terms
- do not mention individual speakers unless unavoidable
- do not turn the overview into conclusions or recommendations

## 10. Opening statements section

This section should use a subheading for each principal leadership speaker.

Default speaker-label format:
- Chairman Rick Scott (R-FL)
- Ranking Member Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY)
- Senator Ashley Moody (R-FL)

Rules:
- in headings, use the full role/title style shown in the submitted memo
- in body text, use short forms such as "Chairman Scott," "Ranking Member Gillibrand," "Sen. Tuberville," "Hon. Yoho," "Mr. Chang," "Ms. Gibson," and "Mr. Harjivan"
- summarize the speaker's framing, concerns, and proposals
- preserve major contrasts between chair and ranking member
- split into two paragraphs when the opening combines framing plus concrete cited actions

## 11. Witnesses Introductions and Testimonies

Each witness receives a named subheading with affiliation.

Preferred heading style:
- Honorable Ted Yoho, Senior Advisor, Kyle House Group
- Mr. Gordon G. Chang, Author of “Plan Red: China’s Project to Destroy America”
- Ms. Rosemary Gibson, Author of “ChinaRX”
- Mr. Chan Harjivan, Visiting Fellow, Duke Margolis Institute for Health Policy

Rules:
- preserve honorifics and titles exactly when verified
- do not guess affiliations
- structure witness summaries around:
  1. main diagnosis or warning
  2. key policy prescriptions or recommendations
- for substantial testimony, prefer two paragraphs rather than one oversized paragraph

## 12. Q&A

Q&A is organized by member, not by issue cluster, in this memo family.

Preferred subsection heading style:
- Senator Tommy Tuberville (R-AL)
- Senator Ashley Moody (R-FL)
- Ranking Member Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY)
- Chairman Rick Scott (R-FL)

Rules:
- group each member's exchanges under one heading
- inside each subsection, summarize the sequence of material questions and answers
- use compact phrasing such as "Sen. Tuberville asked..." / "Mr. Chang replied..."
- do not preserve transcript cadence, filler, or procedural interruptions
- if the chair's closing summary is significant, place it at the end of the chair's subsection instead of creating a new top-level section

## 13. Sentence-level style

Preferred moves:
- "The hearing examined..."
- "The discussion centered on..."
- "He argued that..."
- "She emphasized that..."
- "He urged..."
- "She warned that..."

Avoid:
- rhetorical flourish
- excessive hedging
- repeated "noted that" in every sentence
- stacked subordinate clauses that slow readability

## 14. Quotes

Paraphrase by default.

Use direct quotation marks only when:
- the phrase is short
- the phrase is especially distinctive
- the phrase is likely to matter politically or rhetorically

Even then, use quotes sparingly.

## 15. Inference discipline

This memo family is primarily descriptive.

Rules:
- do not add your own policy implications
- do not predict legislative outcomes
- do not claim consensus without support
- do not characterize testimony as bipartisan, unprecedented, or decisive unless the record clearly supports that description

## 16. Length discipline

Target ranges:
- Hearing Overview: 110 to 200 words
- each opening speaker: 150 to 300 words
- each witness: 150 to 350 words, with flexibility for high-density testimony
- each Q&A member block: 150 to 400 words (longer when multiple questions or rounds are combined)

The target is readable compression, not extreme brevity. A typical memo should run 6 to 10 pages.
Each Q&A entry should capture the specific substance of each question and answer exchange — name the policies, proposals, events, and commitments discussed.

## 17. Dates and factual consistency

Rules:
- hearing date, memo date, and day-of-week must all be internally checked
- if source metadata conflicts, flag for review
- do not silently normalize conflicts unless the workflow explicitly permits metadata repair
- preserve the distinction between memo date and hearing date

## 18. Confidentiality footer

Default confidentiality text:
- Confidential - Not for Public Consumption or Distribution

Rules:
- the memo body should contain the footer text once
- page-level repetition belongs to export formatting in the app, not to content generation
- if another organization-approved confidentiality line is provided, use that instead

## 19. Things the model must not do

- do not invent a separate conclusion section
- do not switch to bullet points unless the product family requests them
- do not shorten official witness titles casually
- do not collapse all leadership statements into one paragraph
- do not turn Q&A into a witness-only summary
- do not output reviewer notes inside the memo body
