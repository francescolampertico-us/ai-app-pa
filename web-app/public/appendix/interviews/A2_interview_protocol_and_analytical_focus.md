# Appendix A2. Interview Protocol and Analytical Focus

## Interview Format

The qualitative component of the capstone used semi-structured interviews. Each conversation followed a common line of inquiry, but questions were adapted to the participant's role, organization, and degree of familiarity with AI tools.

The interviews were conducted to move from general literature claims toward practical workflow knowledge. The aim was not to collect standardized survey data, but to understand how professionals described:

- the actual structure of their work
- the repetitive and judgment-heavy parts of that work
- current AI use in organizations and personal practice
- perceived limits, risks, and adoption barriers
- areas where AI support would be most useful or most problematic

## Recurring Question Areas

### 1. Role, Responsibilities, and Workflow

Participants were first asked to explain their role, day-to-day responsibilities, and the main workflows that shaped a normal week. Follow-up questions often focused on:

- advising versus execution
- internal versus external work
- repetitive or administrative tasks
- tasks that required interpretation, trust, or contextual judgment

### 2. Current AI Use and Organizational Integration

Participants were then asked how AI was currently being used in their own work and in their organizations. This included:

- whether AI use was encouraged, tolerated, or resisted
- whether it was informal, team-based, or organization-wide
- what tools or subscriptions were in use
- whether experimentation, training, or internal guidance existed

### 3. High-Value Use Cases

Particular attention was given to the kinds of tasks where AI already appeared useful in practice. Across interviews, these questions often led to discussion of:

- research compression
- monitoring and synthesis
- first drafts
- memo preparation
- graphic and content iteration
- internal knowledge retrieval

### 4. Limits, Risk, and Resistance

Participants were also asked where AI use became difficult, unreliable, or professionally risky. These discussions frequently touched on:

- factual accuracy and verification
- contractual restrictions
- client trust
- confidentiality and data handling
- missing context
- the limits of AI in high-stakes advisory work

### 5. Future Possibilities

Finally, participants were asked to reflect on where AI might become more valuable in the future. These responses often moved beyond routine task support and toward:

- strategic testing
- political or stakeholder simulation
- AI reputation and visibility
- personalization through knowledge bases or style adaptation
- more advanced decision-support environments

## Supplementary Notes

In addition to the formal interviews, the capstone was informed by supplementary notes from classroom discussion, guest lectures, informal conversations, and direct professional exposure through work in the field. These notes were used to sharpen the framing of the project, but were kept analytically distinct from the formal interview base.

## Analytical Use in the Capstone

The interview material was reviewed qualitatively to identify recurring themes across the conversations. In practical terms, the analysis focused on:

- repeated workflow bottlenecks
- repeated descriptions of where AI currently creates value
- repeated concerns about trust, verification, and professional judgment
- recurring claims about future strategic applications
- implications for tool selection and system design

The interviews did not replace the literature review. Instead, they refined the practical meaning of the literature-supported application areas and helped determine which functions were most appropriate for implementation in the prototype.
