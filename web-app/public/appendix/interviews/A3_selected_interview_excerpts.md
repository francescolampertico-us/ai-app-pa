## 3. Limits in Practice

### Jeffrey Shapiro, Tiber Creek Group

> One thing was wrong. This is not a bat-.300-and-get-to-the-Hall-of-Fame type of job. You have to be right.

### Anthony LaFauce, Clyde

> It's all about risk ... so the higher the level of risk, the more I need to be involved.

### Laura Uttley, Woodwell Climate Research Center

Summary note: Laura repeatedly emphasized the risk of over-reliance, especially where AI output might be accepted without sufficient source validation, contextual understanding, or review.

### Jeffrey Shapiro, Tiber Creek Group

Summary note: Jeffrey described advisory work as highly contextual, stressing that value lies not only in gathering information but in interpreting what matters in Congress, the administration, and the wider policy environment.

## 4. Future Opportunities

### Anthony LaFauce, Clyde

Summary note: Anthony described growing interest in knowledge-based policy systems, digital duplicates, and more advanced AI tools that can support targeted strategic analysis.

### Craig Johnson, Unfiltered Media / Change Agent

Summary note: Craig framed the next frontier less as simple task automation and more as message testing, strategic filtering, and understanding what communication approaches actually move audiences.

### Grace Dahl, Signal Group (supplementary class note)

Summary note: In the AI and Politics class, Grace Dahl described AI as both a tool and a channel, emphasizing AI reputation, persona-based testing, and visibility gaps as emerging issues for Public Affairs strategy.

## 5. Personalization and Adaptation to Practice

### Mike Panetta, Beekeeper Group

> Some clients use healthcare as one word. Some clients use health care as two words. If we're doing that with AI, make sure it's trained to know how the client talks about it the right way.

### Craig Johnson, Unfiltered Media / Change Agent

Summary note: Craig argued that stronger AI performance depends on moving beyond generic systems and toward locally hosted or more customized tools that can better reflect specific styles, constraints, and ways of working.

### Anthony LaFauce, Clyde

Summary note: Anthony described more senior use of AI in terms of asking highly specific questions and creating “digital duplicates” by drawing on notes, emails, and accumulated professional knowledge.
